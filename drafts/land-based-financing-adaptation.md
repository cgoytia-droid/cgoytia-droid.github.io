## 1. Drafted subsection (paper-ready prose)

### Land-based financing as the bridge from adaptation to bankability

The urban adaptation finance gap is, at its core, less a shortage of capital
than a shortage of the institutional conditions that let capital flow. Against
estimated developing-country adaptation needs of US$140–300 billion per year
(UNEP, 2023) — and an urban climate finance gap on the order of US$4.3 trillion
in needs against roughly US$831 billion in flows (Karapantsiou et al., 2026) —
the binding constraint for most cities is the absence of *bankable* projects:
investments structured so that lenders and investors can finance them against a
predictable, ring-fenced revenue stream (C40 Cities, 2023). Land-based
financing (LBF) speaks directly to this constraint. Its premise is a three-step
causal chain: well-targeted adaptation reduces climate risk and enhances
amenity; that risk reduction and amenity are capitalised into surrounding land
and property values; and a share of the resulting uplift can be recovered
through fiscal instruments — recurrent property taxes, stormwater and water
tariffs, betterment levies, development charges, and the sale of building rights
(OECD & Lincoln Institute of Land Policy, 2022; Smolka, 2013).

Crucially, this is not merely theoretical potential. Realised and modelled
evidence across diverse institutional settings demonstrates both the revenue
and its limits. Miami Beach raised more than US$500 million for road elevation
and stormwater management through an impervious-area stormwater fee, against
which the city is authorised to issue revenue bonds. Copenhagen finances the
bulk of a roughly €1.3 billion cloudburst programme through water tariffs and
recovers, via the property tax, a share of an estimated €188 million in
property-value uplift generated by its blue-green works. In Buenos Aires, a
US$338 million flood-mitigation programme is modelled to have created
approximately US$379 million in net new land value — arising primarily from
general-equilibrium effects, as reduced risk made central locations attractive
for densification and in-migration — although that value has not yet been
institutionally captured (Avner et al., 2022). At the constrained end, Lagos
and Ho Chi Minh City confront annual flood damages of US$4 billion and
US$354 million respectively, yet raise little or no land-based revenue: Lagos's
consolidated property charge yields about US$0.43 per capita at roughly 30%
compliance, and Vietnam levies no recurrent property tax. The lesson is
consistent: where adaptation reduces risk in a transparent property market, it
creates a capturable fiscal base; where cadastres, valuation, and risk
disclosure are weak, the same economics fail to translate into revenue. **The
binding constraint on land-based adaptation finance is institutional, not
technical.**

The deeper significance of land-based revenue lies in what it unlocks. A
predictable, ring-fenced LBF stream is precisely the attribute that converts an
adaptation project from a budget line into a bankable asset: it supplies the
repayment source a green or sustainability-linked bond requires, and the
own-source revenue against which a city can negotiate concessional or blended
finance from the Green Climate Fund, the Inter-American Development Bank, CAF,
or the World Bank. LBF is therefore not a substitute for international climate
finance but a precondition for it — raising domestic revenue while building the
very cadastral, valuation, and disclosure institutions that raise a city's
capacity to absorb external capital. This reframes the policy task as a
*readiness sequence*: risk information, then capture institutions, then
leverage, each stage a prerequisite for the next. Cities cannot capture value
that markets do not price, cannot pledge revenue they cannot reliably collect,
and cannot leverage a stream that is not ring-fenced.

Finally, the equity of capture is a test of legitimacy, not a footnote to
efficiency. Because land-based finance recovers most where land is most
valuable, its capturable base concentrates in higher-income areas, while the
largest well-being losses from climate shocks fall on the poor, who are
disproportionately settled in the hazardous, market-discounted locations that
generate little revenue (Hallegatte et al., 2017). Yet who pays and who
benefits are separable decisions. A ring-fenced vehicle that captures value
where it is created and disburses it according to a needs-based rule — for
instance, a municipal climate-adaptation trust fund whose allocation is
governed by a vulnerability index prioritising neighbourhoods with the lowest
baseline protection — makes land-based finance itself redistributive. The
design principle is to **capture where value is highest and invest where need
is greatest.**

## 2. Reusable building blocks (for splicing into the host paper)

### Headline figures
- Adaptation finance gap: **US$140–300 bn/yr** for developing countries (UNEP, 2023).
- Urban gap: **~US$4.3 tn needs vs ~US$831 bn flows** (Karapantsiou et al., 2026).
- **1.81 bn** people in flood-prone areas; **~600 m** urban; up to **10%** of city jobs flood-affected (World Bank, 2026).

### City evidence (drop-in)

|---|---|---|---|---|
| Copenhagen | ≈ €1.3 bn cloudburst | Mostly water tariffs (realised); ≈ €188 m uplift, share via property tax | 3.0–6.9 | 6% discount (100-yr) |
| Miami-Dade | > US$500 m (Miami Beach) | > US$500 m stormwater fee (realised); 15–25% CRS discounts | 2.33–3.04 | 3–5% overvaluation |
| Buenos Aires | US$338 m (World Bank) | ≈ US$379 m net land-value gain (modelled); not captured | self-financing | 4.3–11% discount |
| Ho Chi Minh City | below need | Land-use fees ≈ 8% of tax revenue; no recurrent property tax | unrealised | 7.5% discount |
| Lagos | < US$1 bn/yr vs > US$50 bn gap | Land Use Charge ≈ US$0.43/capita; ≈ 30% compliance | unrealised | risk un-priced |

### Transferable frameworks
1. **Five criteria for a "well-designed" investment**: value creation → capture mechanism → cost-effectiveness (BCR > 1) → institutional feasibility → equity.
2. **Value preservation vs value creation** (spatial-equilibrium urban economics; Avner et al., 2022; Brueckner, 2011).
3. **Readiness sequence**: risk information → capture institutions → leverage, mapped to a four-tier typology (mature / innovative middle-income / emerging / constrained).
